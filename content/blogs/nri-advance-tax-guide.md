# NRI Advance Tax -- When, How Much & How to Pay (FY 2025-26)

**By CA Mayank Wadhera (CA | CS | CMA | IBBI Registered Valuer)**
*MKW Advisors | Legal Suvidha | DigiComply*

*Last Updated: 23 March 2026 | Applicable for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27)*

---

Most NRIs assume that once TDS is deducted from their Indian income, their tax obligations are settled. This assumption works in many cases -- but not always. When your estimated tax liability for the financial year, after accounting for all TDS credits, exceeds Rs. 10,000, you are required to pay **advance tax** in quarterly installments.

Failure to pay advance tax -- or paying it late -- triggers interest under **Section 234B** and **Section 234C** of the Income Tax Act. These charges are automatic, non-negotiable, and calculated at 1% per month. Over a full year, that adds up to 12% simple interest on the shortfall.

This guide walks you through the entire advance tax process for NRIs: who needs to pay, how to calculate the amount, the exact installment schedule, how to make payment online, and how to avoid common and costly mistakes.

<a id="what-is-advance-tax"></a>
## 1. What Is Advance Tax and Why Does It Matter for NRIs?

Advance tax is a "pay-as-you-earn" mechanism under the Indian Income Tax Act. Instead of paying the entire year's tax liability in a lump sum after the financial year ends, taxpayers are required to estimate their annual income and pay tax on it in installments during the year itself.

The legal basis is found in **Sections 207, 208, and 209** of the Income Tax Act, 1961:

- **Section 207**: Every person whose estimated tax liability for the year is Rs. 10,000 or more shall pay advance tax.
- **Section 208**: Advance tax is payable if the estimated tax liability, after reducing TDS/TCS credits, is Rs. 10,000 or more.
- **Section 209**: Prescribes the method for computing advance tax liability.

**Important exception for senior citizens**: Resident senior citizens (60 years or older) with no income from business or profession are exempt from advance tax under Section 207. However, this exemption does **not** apply to NRIs. If you are an NRI, regardless of your age, advance tax provisions apply to you.

For NRIs, the most common scenarios triggering advance tax are:

- Property sale where TDS deducted is less than the actual capital gains tax
- Rental income where the tenant has not deducted TDS
- Capital gains from equity, mutual funds, or other securities
- Interest income from NRO accounts exceeding TDS coverage (rare, but possible with multiple income sources)
- Professional or consulting income received from India

<a id="when-does-advance-tax-apply"></a>
## 2. When Does Advance Tax Apply to an NRI?

The threshold is simple and definitive:

**If your estimated tax liability for FY 2025-26, after subtracting all TDS and TCS credits, is Rs. 10,000 or more, you must pay advance tax.**

Here is how to determine whether you fall within this requirement:

### Step 1: Estimate Your Total Indian Income

Add up all income that is taxable in India for the financial year:
- Capital gains from property, shares, mutual funds
- Rental income from Indian property
- Interest income from NRO fixed deposits and savings accounts
- Professional or consulting fees received from Indian clients
- Any other income sourced in India

### Step 2: Compute Tax on This Income

Apply the applicable tax slab rates or special rates (such as LTCG at 12.5%, STCG at 20% for equity, or slab rates for other income). Add surcharge and health & education cess as applicable.

### Step 3: Subtract TDS/TCS Credits

Deduct all TDS that has been or will be deducted during the year -- TDS on NRO interest, TDS on property sale (Section 194IA), TDS on rent (Section 194IB), and any other TDS.

### Step 4: Check the Rs. 10,000 Threshold

If the net tax payable (Step 2 minus Step 3) exceeds Rs. 10,000, advance tax is mandatory.

**Example**: Your estimated tax for FY 2025-26 is Rs. 3,20,000. TDS already deducted or expected is Rs. 2,85,000. Net payable = Rs. 35,000. Since Rs. 35,000 > Rs. 10,000, you must pay advance tax.

<a id="installment-schedule"></a>
## 3. The 4-Installment Schedule for FY 2025-26

Advance tax for FY 2025-26 is payable in four installments. Each installment has a due date and a cumulative percentage:

| Installment | Due Date | Cumulative % of Tax | What You Pay |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | 15 June 2025 | 15% | 15% of estimated annual tax |
| 2nd | 15 September 2025 | 45% | 30% of estimated annual tax (45% minus 15% already paid) |
| 3rd | 15 December 2025 | 75% | 30% of estimated annual tax (75% minus 45% already paid) |
| 4th | 15 March 2026 | 100% | 25% of estimated annual tax (100% minus 75% already paid) |

### Key Points About the Schedule

**Cumulative calculation**: Each installment date specifies the cumulative percentage that should have been paid by that date -- not the individual installment amount. If you miss the first installment, you should catch up by paying 45% by the second due date.

**Capital gains exception**: Capital gains and certain incomes that arise after a particular installment date do not attract advance tax for the installments already passed. For example, if you sell a property in October 2025 (Q3), you are not required to have paid advance tax on that gain in the June and September installments. Instead, you pay the full tax on such gains in the remaining installments. This is the **Section 234C proviso** -- more on this later.

**Weekends and holidays**: If the due date falls on a Sunday or a public holiday, the payment can be made on the next working day without penalty.

<a id="estimate-tax-liability"></a>
## 4. How to Estimate Your Tax Liability Mid-Year

Estimating tax liability mid-year is the most challenging part of advance tax compliance. Here is a structured approach:

### For Regular/Recurring Income (Rent, Interest)

These are relatively predictable. Take your monthly or quarterly rental income or NRO interest and extrapolate for the full year.

**Rental income estimate**: Monthly rent x 12, minus standard deduction of 30% under Section 24(a), minus municipal taxes paid. Apply slab rates to the net amount.

**NRO interest estimate**: Check your FD maturity schedule. Banks deduct TDS at 30% (plus surcharge and cess) on NRO interest, which usually covers the full tax liability. But if your total income pushes you into higher surcharge brackets, a differential may arise.

### For One-Time/Event-Based Income (Property Sale, Share Sale)

These are harder to predict because they depend on transactions that may or may not occur. The rule of thumb:

- **Before the transaction**: Do not include speculative gains in your advance tax estimate
- **After the transaction**: Immediately compute the capital gain and pay advance tax in the next available installment

### Revised Estimates

You are permitted -- and encouraged -- to revise your advance tax estimate at each installment date. If your income turns out to be higher or lower than initially estimated, adjust the subsequent installments accordingly.

### Quick Computation Framework

Use this formula at each installment date:

```
Advance Tax Due = (Estimated Total Tax on Indian Income)
                  - (Total TDS/TCS Credits Expected)
                  - (Advance Tax Already Paid in Earlier Installments)
```

Multiply the net estimated annual advance tax by the applicable cumulative percentage (15%, 45%, 75%, or 100%) and subtract any advance tax already paid.

<a id="property-sale"></a>
## 5. Advance Tax on Property Sale -- Is TDS Enough?

This is one of the most misunderstood areas of NRI taxation.

### The TDS Mechanism on Property Sale by NRI

When an NRI sells property in India, the buyer is required to deduct TDS under **Section 195** (not Section 194IA, which applies to resident sellers). The TDS rate depends on the type of capital gain:

- **Long-term capital gain (property held > 24 months)**: TDS at 12.5% of the sale consideration (not just the capital gain)
- **Short-term capital gain (property held < 24 months)**: TDS at applicable slab rates on the sale consideration

### When TDS May Not Be Enough

The problem arises because TDS is deducted on the **full sale consideration**, not on the capital gain. In most cases, TDS at 12.5% of the sale consideration significantly exceeds the actual LTCG tax (12.5% of just the capital gain). So for long-term property sales, TDS usually more than covers the tax.

However, TDS may fall short in these situations:

1. **Lower TDS certificate (Section 197)**: If you obtained a lower TDS certificate from the Income Tax Department, the buyer deducts TDS at a reduced rate. The difference between your actual tax and the lower TDS must be covered by advance tax.
2. **Short-term capital gain with high profit margin**: If the gain is substantial and pushes you into higher slab/surcharge brackets.
3. **Buyer fails to deduct correct TDS**: Some buyers, especially individuals unfamiliar with NRI transactions, may incorrectly apply Section 194IA rates (1% for property above Rs. 50 lakhs) instead of Section 195 rates.

### What You Should Do

After any property sale, immediately compute the actual capital gains tax. Compare it with the TDS deducted. If there is a shortfall exceeding Rs. 10,000 (considering your other income and TDS), pay advance tax in the next installment.

> **Pro Tip**: If you are selling property mid-year, get your capital gains computation done by a qualified CA before the next advance tax due date. This prevents interest charges under Section 234B and 234C.

<a id="rental-income"></a>
## 6. Advance Tax on Rental Income

Rental income is one of the most common triggers for advance tax obligations among NRIs. Here is why.

### The TDS Gap on Rental Income

Under **Section 194IB**, tenants paying rent exceeding Rs. 50,000 per month are required to deduct TDS at 2% on rent paid to a resident landlord -- but at **31.2%** (30% + 4% cess) when paying rent to an NRI landlord under Section 195.

The problem? Many tenants do not know their landlord is an NRI. They either:
- Do not deduct TDS at all
- Deduct TDS at 2% under Section 194IB (the resident rate) instead of the NRI rate under Section 195

In either case, the NRI landlord has a significant tax shortfall that must be covered through advance tax.

### Computation for Rental Income

Here is the step-by-step process:

1. **Gross Annual Rent**: Monthly rent x 12 = Gross Annual Value
2. **Less Municipal Taxes**: Deduct property taxes actually paid by you
3. **Net Annual Value**: Gross Annual Value minus Municipal Taxes
4. **Standard Deduction**: 30% of Net Annual Value (under Section 24(a))
5. **Interest on Home Loan**: Deduct interest paid on any loan taken for this property (Section 24(b))
6. **Taxable Rental Income**: Net Annual Value minus Standard Deduction minus Home Loan Interest
7. **Tax on Rental Income**: Apply slab rates to this amount
8. **Less TDS Credits**: Subtract any TDS deducted by the tenant
9. **Net Advance Tax Payable**: If this exceeds Rs. 10,000, pay in installments

### Practical Scenario

Monthly rent received: Rs. 60,000. Annual: Rs. 7,20,000. After 30% standard deduction: Rs. 5,04,000. Tax at slab rates (assuming no other Indian income): approximately Rs. 25,200 (under new regime). If tenant has not deducted any TDS, the full Rs. 25,200 is payable as advance tax.

<a id="capital-gains"></a>
## 7. Advance Tax on Capital Gains from Shares and Mutual Funds

Capital gains from equity and mutual fund transactions are another major area where NRIs face advance tax obligations.

### Tax Rates on Capital Gains (FY 2025-26)

| Asset Type | Holding Period for LTCG | LTCG Rate | STCG Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Listed equity shares | > 12 months | 12.5% (above Rs. 1.25 lakh exemption) | 20% |
| Equity mutual funds | > 12 months | 12.5% (above Rs. 1.25 lakh exemption) | 20% |
| Debt mutual funds | > 24 months | 12.5% | Slab rates |
| Unlisted shares | > 24 months | 12.5% | Slab rates |

### TDS on Capital Gains for NRIs

When an NRI sells shares or mutual fund units, the depository participant, broker, or fund house deducts TDS:

- **LTCG on equity**: TDS at 12.5% on the capital gain
- **STCG on equity**: TDS at 20% on the capital gain
- **Debt fund redemptions**: TDS at 12.5% or 30% depending on holding period

In most cases, TDS on capital gains closely matches the actual tax liability. However, advance tax arises when:

1. **Multiple transactions across different brokers**: TDS computation on each individual transaction may not account for the Rs. 1.25 lakh LTCG exemption correctly, or surcharge may not be applied at each point.
2. **Trading through foreign brokers**: Some international brokerage platforms do not deduct Indian TDS. The full tax on gains must then be paid as advance tax.
3. **Futures and Options (F&O) income**: Classified as business income, taxed at slab rates, and no TDS is usually deducted.

### Timing Rule for Capital Gains

The **Section 234C proviso** provides relief: if capital gains arise after a particular advance tax installment date, you are not liable for interest on non-payment for the installments already passed. You must include the tax on such capital gains in the remaining installments.

For example, if you sell equity shares in November 2025, the capital gains tax must be factored into the 3rd installment (December 15) and 4th installment (March 15). You will not face Section 234C interest for the June and September installments.

<a id="tds-credit"></a>
## 8. How TDS Credit Reduces Your Advance Tax Obligation

TDS is not a separate tax -- it is an advance collection of income tax. Every rupee of TDS deducted from your income reduces your advance tax obligation rupee for rupee.

### The Reduction Mechanism

```
Advance Tax Payable = Total Estimated Tax - Total TDS Credits

If the result is < Rs. 10,000 --> No advance tax required
If the result is >= Rs. 10,000 --> Pay advance tax in installments
```

### Practical Application

Consider an NRI with three income streams in FY 2025-26:

| Income Source | Amount | TDS Deducted | Tax Liability |
|---|---|---|---|
| NRO FD Interest | Rs. 8,00,000 | Rs. 2,49,600 (31.2%) | Rs. 2,49,600 |
| Rental Income | Rs. 7,20,000 | Rs. 0 (tenant unaware) | Rs. 1,56,000 |
| LTCG on Shares | Rs. 3,50,000 | Rs. 28,125 (12.5% on Rs. 2,25,000 after exemption) | Rs. 28,125 |

- Total estimated tax: Rs. 4,33,725 (approximate, before surcharge/cess adjustments)
- Total TDS: Rs. 2,77,725
- Net advance tax payable: Rs. 1,56,000
- Since Rs. 1,56,000 > Rs. 10,000, advance tax is mandatory

The NRI must pay Rs. 1,56,000 in four installments as per the schedule.

### Verifying TDS Credits

Always verify TDS credits through your **Form 26AS** and **Annual Information Statement (AIS)** on the Income Tax portal. Mismatched or unreported TDS is a common source of errors. If TDS has been deducted but is not reflecting in Form 26AS, follow up with the deductor to file or correct their TDS return.

<a id="pay-online"></a>
## 9. How to Pay Advance Tax Online (e-Pay Tax Portal)

The process for paying advance tax has been fully digitized. Here is the step-by-step procedure using the **e-Pay Tax** facility on the Income Tax Department's portal.

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1: Visit the e-Filing Portal**
Go to [https://www.incometax.gov.in](https://www.incometax.gov.in). You can pay with or without logging in.

**Step 2: Navigate to e-Pay Tax**
Click on "Quick Links" on the homepage and select "e-Pay Tax." Alternatively, go to e-File > e-Pay Tax after logging in.

**Step 3: Enter PAN Details**
Enter your PAN number and mobile number. An OTP will be sent for verification.

**Step 4: Select "Income Tax" as Payment Type**
After OTP verification, you will see payment categories. Select **"Income Tax"**.

**Step 5: Select Assessment Year**
For FY 2025-26, select **AY 2026-27**.

**Step 6: Select Type of Payment**
Choose **"Advance Tax (100)"** from the dropdown. This is the equivalent of the old Challan 280 with the minor head code 100.

- Minor Head 100 = Advance Tax
- Minor Head 300 = Self-Assessment Tax
- Minor Head 400 = Tax on Regular Assessment

**Step 7: Enter Tax Amount**
Enter the advance tax amount for the current installment. You may also fill in surcharge and cess separately if needed.

**Step 8: Select Payment Method**
Choose from:
- Net Banking (all major banks supported)
- Debit Card
- Pay at Bank Counter (generates a challan for over-the-counter payment)
- NEFT/RTGS
- UPI

**Step 9: Complete Payment**
After payment, a **Challan Identification Number (CIN)** or **BSR Code** is generated. Save this receipt -- you will need it when filing your income tax return.

### Important Notes for NRIs

- You do **not** need an Indian bank account to pay online. NEFT/RTGS from overseas accounts to the tax-collecting branch is possible, though practically complex. Most NRIs use their NRE/NRO account linked to Indian net banking.
- If you are unable to access Indian net banking from abroad, authorize a trusted person in India through a Power of Attorney to make the payment.
- The old physical Challan 280 system has been largely replaced by e-Pay Tax, but some bank branches still accept physical challans.

### After Payment

- Verify the payment in your **Form 26AS** within 3-5 working days
- Cross-check the amount and assessment year
- Save the challan receipt for your records

<a id="sat-vs-advance"></a>
## 10. Self-Assessment Tax vs. Advance Tax -- Key Differences

NRIs often confuse these two types of tax payments. Here is a clear comparison:

| Parameter | Advance Tax | Self-Assessment Tax |
|---|---|---|
| **When it is paid** | During the financial year, before year-end | After the financial year ends, before filing return |
| **Purpose** | Pay tax as income is earned | Pay remaining tax after accounting for TDS + advance tax |
| **Minor Head Code** | 100 | 300 |
| **Installments** | 4 installments (Jun, Sep, Dec, Mar) | Single payment |
| **Interest on default** | Section 234B + 234C | Section 234A (for late filing) |
| **Legal basis** | Sections 207-211 | Section 140A |

### When Self-Assessment Tax Is Needed

Even after paying advance tax, there may be a residual tax liability. This happens when:

- Your actual income turned out to be higher than estimated
- You underestimated capital gains or rental income
- TDS credits were lower than expected

In such cases, pay the balance as self-assessment tax before filing your return. If you pay the differential before the return filing deadline, Section 234B interest is calculated only up to the date of filing, and at reduced rates compared to a complete non-payment scenario.

<a id="interest-penalties"></a>
## 11. Interest for Non-Payment: Section 234B and Section 234C

The Income Tax Act levies two types of interest for advance tax defaults. Both are mandatory -- the Assessing Officer has no discretion to waive them.

### Section 234B -- Interest on Shortfall of Advance Tax

**Trigger**: If the total advance tax paid during the year is less than 90% of the assessed tax (tax as computed in your return or assessment).

**Rate**: 1% per month (simple interest) on the shortfall amount.

**Period**: From 1st April of the assessment year (i.e., 1 April 2026 for FY 2025-26) until the date of payment of the shortfall (or date of determination of income by assessment).

**Computation**:

```
Shortfall = Assessed Tax - Advance Tax Paid
Interest = Shortfall x 1% x Number of Months (part of a month = full month)
```

**Example**: Assessed tax = Rs. 5,00,000. Advance tax paid = Rs. 4,00,000. Shortfall = Rs. 1,00,000. If you pay the balance as self-assessment tax on 15 July 2026, interest = Rs. 1,00,000 x 1% x 4 months (April to July) = Rs. 4,000.

### Section 234C -- Interest on Deferment of Advance Tax Installments

**Trigger**: If you do not pay the required percentage of advance tax by each installment due date.

**Rate**: 1% per month (simple interest) on the shortfall for each installment.

**Period**: 3 months per installment shortfall (for the first three installments) and 1 month for the last installment.

**Computation for each installment**:

| Installment | Due Date | Required Cumulative % | Interest Period |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | 15 June | 15% | 3 months |
| 2nd | 15 September | 45% | 3 months |
| 3rd | 15 December | 75% | 3 months |
| 4th | 15 March | 100% | 1 month |

**Example**: Estimated tax = Rs. 4,00,000. By 15 June, you should pay Rs. 60,000 (15%) but pay nothing.

- Shortfall for 1st installment: Rs. 60,000
- Interest under 234C: Rs. 60,000 x 1% x 3 = Rs. 1,800

**Capital gains relief under Section 234C**: If capital gains or income of an unpredictable nature arises after a particular installment date, you are not charged 234C interest for the installments that preceded the income event. You must include such income in the immediately following and subsequent installments.

### Combined Impact

For an NRI who completely ignores advance tax, the combined interest under 234B and 234C can amount to 12-15% of the unpaid tax over the year. This is a significant cost that is entirely avoidable with proper planning.

<a id="practical-example"></a>
## 12. Practical Example: Complete Advance Tax Computation

Let us work through a realistic scenario for an NRI in FY 2025-26.

### Profile

**Rajesh**, an NRI based in Dubai, has the following Indian income during FY 2025-26:

1. **Property sale in August 2025 (Q2)**: Sells a flat in Mumbai for Rs. 1,20,00,000. Indexed cost of acquisition: Rs. 85,00,000. LTCG = Rs. 35,00,000. Buyer deducts TDS at 12.5% of sale consideration = Rs. 15,00,000 under Section 195.
2. **Rental income throughout the year**: Rs. 45,000/month from a second property. Tenant does not deduct TDS (unaware landlord is NRI). Annual rent = Rs. 5,40,000.
3. **NRO FD interest**: Rs. 3,00,000 during the year. Bank deducts TDS at 31.2% = Rs. 93,600.

### Step 1: Compute Total Taxable Income

| Income Head | Gross | Deductions | Taxable |
|---|---|---|---|
| LTCG on Property | Rs. 35,00,000 | Nil | Rs. 35,00,000 |
| Rental Income | Rs. 5,40,000 | Rs. 1,62,000 (30% std. deduction) | Rs. 3,78,000 |
| NRO Interest | Rs. 3,00,000 | Nil | Rs. 3,00,000 |
| **Total** | | | **Rs. 41,78,000** |

### Step 2: Compute Tax Liability

- LTCG on property: Rs. 35,00,000 x 12.5% = Rs. 4,37,500
- Rental + Interest (Rs. 6,78,000) taxed at slab rates (new regime): approximately Rs. 44,600
- Total tax before cess: Rs. 4,82,100
- Health & Education Cess at 4%: Rs. 19,284
- **Total tax liability: Rs. 5,01,384** (approximately)

### Step 3: Subtract TDS Credits

| Source | TDS |
|---|---|
| Property sale (Section 195) | Rs. 15,00,000 |
| NRO FD interest | Rs. 93,600 |
| Rental income | Rs. 0 |
| **Total TDS** | **Rs. 15,93,600** |

### Step 4: Net Tax Position

Net tax = Rs. 5,01,384 - Rs. 15,93,600 = **Negative Rs. 10,92,216**

**Result**: Rajesh does NOT need to pay advance tax. In fact, he has excess TDS of approximately Rs. 10,92,216, which he can claim as a refund when filing his income tax return.

### Why This Happens

The TDS on property sale by an NRI is computed on the **entire sale consideration** (Rs. 1,20,00,000 x 12.5% = Rs. 15,00,000), not on the capital gain (Rs. 35,00,000). This typically results in a massive over-deduction.

### Modified Scenario -- When Advance Tax IS Required

Now let us assume Rajesh obtained a **lower TDS certificate** under Section 197, and the buyer deducted TDS at only Rs. 4,00,000 (based on estimated LTCG). Additionally, the tenant on his rental property also does not deduct TDS.

| Source | TDS |
|---|---|
| Property sale (lower certificate) | Rs. 4,00,000 |
| NRO FD interest | Rs. 93,600 |
| Rental income | Rs. 0 |
| **Total TDS** | **Rs. 4,93,600** |

Net tax = Rs. 5,01,384 - Rs. 4,93,600 = **Rs. 7,784**

**Result**: Net tax payable is Rs. 7,784, which is less than Rs. 10,000. Advance tax is still NOT required. Rajesh can pay this as self-assessment tax when filing his return.

### Second Modified Scenario -- Advance Tax IS Required

Same facts, but Rajesh also sells equity shares with LTCG of Rs. 15,00,000 in October 2025. Tax on equity LTCG: (Rs. 15,00,000 - Rs. 1,25,000 exemption) x 12.5% = Rs. 1,71,875 + 4% cess = Rs. 1,78,750. Broker deducts TDS of Rs. 1,71,875.

Updated total tax: Rs. 5,01,384 + Rs. 1,78,750 = Rs. 6,80,134
Updated total TDS: Rs. 4,93,600 + Rs. 1,71,875 = Rs. 6,65,475
Net payable: Rs. 14,659

**Since Rs. 14,659 > Rs. 10,000, advance tax is required.**

Since both the property sale (August) and equity sale (October) occur after June 15, no advance tax is due in the 1st installment. The schedule would be:

- **By 15 September**: 45% of Rs. 14,659 = Rs. 6,597 (property gain occurred in Q2)
- **By 15 December**: 75% of Rs. 14,659 = Rs. 10,994 minus Rs. 6,597 = Rs. 4,397 (equity gain occurred in Q3)
- **By 15 March**: 100% of Rs. 14,659 minus Rs. 10,994 = Rs. 3,665

<a id="common-mistakes"></a>
## 13. Common Mistakes NRIs Make with Advance Tax

### Mistake 1: Not Paying Advance Tax on Rental Income

This is the most frequent error. Many NRI landlords collect rent year after year without paying any tax, assuming TDS covers everything. When the tenant does not deduct TDS (which is common), the entire tax liability remains unpaid, accumulating interest under Sections 234B and 234C.

**Fix**: At the start of each financial year, estimate your rental income tax and set up advance tax payments.

### Mistake 2: Paying All Tax After the Sale Instead of in Installments

NRIs who sell property often pay the entire differential tax as self-assessment tax after the financial year ends. While this settles the principal tax, they still incur Section 234B and 234C interest for the months of non-payment.

**Fix**: Pay advance tax in the installment immediately following the sale transaction.

### Mistake 3: Assuming TDS on Property Sale Always Covers the Tax

As shown in our practical example, when a lower TDS certificate is obtained, the actual TDS may be significantly less than the tax liability. NRIs who obtain Section 197 certificates must be especially vigilant about advance tax.

**Fix**: After obtaining a lower TDS certificate, compute the gap between estimated tax and reduced TDS, and pay the difference as advance tax.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Capital Gains from Mutual Fund Redemptions

NRIs often redeem mutual fund units without tracking the capital gains tax implications. While fund houses deduct TDS, the rates and computation may not perfectly match the final liability (especially when aggregating multiple redemptions for the Rs. 1.25 lakh LTCG exemption).

**Fix**: Maintain a running tracker of all mutual fund redemptions and capital gains during the year.

### Mistake 5: Missing the March 15 Deadline

The 4th installment deadline of March 15 catches many NRIs off guard, especially those who procrastinate on year-end tax planning. Missing this date by even one day triggers Section 234C interest for one month.

**Fix**: Set calendar reminders for all four advance tax due dates.

### Mistake 6: Not Verifying TDS in Form 26AS

Paying advance tax based on assumed TDS credits that never actually reflect in Form 26AS leads to mismatches during assessment. The department may raise demands for unpaid tax, including interest.

**Fix**: Check Form 26AS quarterly to confirm that all TDS deducted is properly reported.

## 14. Frequently Asked Questions

### Q1: I am an NRI with only NRO FD interest income. Do I need to pay advance tax?

Banks deduct TDS at 30% (plus surcharge and cess) on NRO interest. For most NRIs, this TDS fully covers the tax liability on interest income. You need to pay advance tax only if you have additional Indian income where TDS is insufficient, causing the net tax payable to exceed Rs. 10,000.

### Q2: Is advance tax applicable under both the old and new tax regimes?

Yes. Advance tax provisions apply regardless of which tax regime you choose. The computation will differ based on the slab rates and deductions available under each regime, but the obligation to pay advance tax remains the same.

### Q3: I sold property in January 2026. Which advance tax installments apply?

Since the sale occurred after the 3rd installment date (December 15, 2025), you need to include the capital gains tax in the 4th installment due by March 15, 2026. You will not face Section 234C interest for the first three installments on this capital gain.

### Q4: Can I pay advance tax after March 15 but before March 31?

Yes, you can pay before March 31, but it will be treated as advance tax only if paid on or before March 15. Payments between March 16 and March 31 are technically still advance tax for calculation purposes, but you will be charged Section 234C interest for the deferment of the 4th installment.

### Q5: What happens if I overestimate my advance tax and pay excess?

The excess amount will be adjusted against your total tax liability when you file your return. Any remaining excess will be refunded by the Income Tax Department, usually with interest under Section 244A at 0.5% per month.

### Q6: I have a DTAA benefit reducing my tax. Should I factor this into advance tax?

Yes. If a Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA) reduces your Indian tax liability (for example, a lower rate on interest income), you should compute advance tax based on the DTAA-reduced rate. Ensure you have the necessary documentation (Tax Residency Certificate from your country of residence) to support the DTAA claim.

### Q7: My tenant deducts TDS at 2% instead of 30%. What should I do?

First, inform your tenant that you are an NRI and that TDS must be deducted under Section 195 at the applicable rate (typically 30% plus surcharge and cess). Second, compute the tax shortfall and pay it as advance tax. Report the correct income in your return.

### Q8: Can I pay advance tax using my NRE account?

Yes, you can pay income tax (including advance tax) from your NRE account. The amount will be debited from your NRE balance and credited to the government. There is no restriction on using NRE funds for tax payments.

### Q9: Is there any penalty beyond interest for not paying advance tax?

No separate penalty is levied for non-payment of advance tax. The consequence is limited to interest under Sections 234B and 234C. However, persistent non-filing and non-payment of tax can lead to scrutiny, notices, and in extreme cases, prosecution proceedings.

### Q10: How do I pay advance tax if I do not have Indian net banking access from abroad?

You have several options: (a) Use your NRE/NRO account's international net banking facility, (b) authorize a family member or trusted person in India via Power of Attorney to make the payment, (c) use the NEFT/RTGS option to transfer funds to the tax-collecting branch, or (d) contact your bank's NRI services desk for assistance.

### Q11: If I become a resident mid-year, does the advance tax calculation change?

Your residential status is determined for the entire financial year, not mid-year. If you are a resident for FY 2025-26, your global income is taxable in India, and advance tax must be computed on total global income. If you are an NRI, only Indian-sourced income is subject to advance tax.

### Q12: Do I need to file any form when paying advance tax?

No separate form is required. You simply make the payment through the e-Pay Tax portal selecting the correct assessment year, payment type (Advance Tax - Code 100), and amount. The challan receipt serves as proof of payment.

## 15. Next Steps

Advance tax compliance is one of those areas where getting it right from the beginning saves significant money and hassle. Interest under Sections 234B and 234C is non-waivable and adds up quickly -- 1% per month on the shortfall is effectively 12% per annum.

Here is what you should do now:

1. **Estimate your FY 2025-26 Indian income** across all sources -- rental, interest, capital gains, professional fees
2. **Compute the tax liability** using applicable rates and regime
3. **Subtract all expected TDS credits** from Form 26AS
4. **If the net exceeds Rs. 10,000**, set up advance tax payments per the installment schedule
5. **Review and revise** at each installment date based on actual income and TDS

If you are unsure about your computation, or if you have complex income streams involving property sales, capital gains, and rental income, professional guidance can prevent costly errors.
