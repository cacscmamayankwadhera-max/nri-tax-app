# NRI Power of Attorney in India — GPA vs SPA, Registration & Property Transactions (2026 Guide)

**By CA Mayank Wadhera** (CA | CS | CMA | IBBI Registered Valuer)

## Why Every NRI Needs to Understand Power of Attorney

You live thousands of miles away. Your property in India needs a sale deed signed at the sub-registrar office. Your NRO account requires a fixed deposit to be broken. Your income tax return needs a physical verification. Your elderly parent cannot manage your mutual fund folio alone.

For millions of NRIs, the Power of Attorney (POA) is the single most critical legal instrument that keeps their Indian affairs running. Yet it is also the instrument most frequently executed incorrectly, leading to rejected registrations, frozen bank accounts, failed property transactions, and in worst cases, unauthorized transfers worth crores.

At MKW Advisors, we review dozens of NRI POA documents every month. The most common words we hear: *"My lawyer abroad said this would work in India."* It often does not — because Indian POA requirements are specific, state-dependent, and governed by rules that differ fundamentally from how POAs work in the US, UK, UAE, or Singapore.

This guide covers everything: what a POA is, GPA versus SPA, the landmark Supreme Court ruling that changed everything, how to execute and register a valid POA from abroad, what it can and cannot do, and the practical steps to protect yourself from misuse.

## What Is a Power of Attorney Under Indian Law?

A Power of Attorney is a legal instrument by which one person (the **Principal** or **Donor**) authorizes another person (the **Agent**, **Attorney**, or **Donee**) to act on their behalf in legal, financial, or property matters.

In India, POA is governed by the **Power of Attorney Act, 1882** — a short statute with just four sections — read in conjunction with:

- **Indian Contract Act, 1872** (Sections 182-238 on Agency)
- **Registration Act, 1908** (Section 33 — who can present documents for registration)
- **Indian Stamp Act, 1899** (stamp duty on POA instruments)
- **Transfer of Property Act, 1882** (property conveyance requirements)

The key legal concept: a POA creates an **agency relationship**. The agent acts on behalf of the principal, and the principal is bound by the agent's authorized acts as if the principal performed them personally.

### Critical Distinction: POA Is Not a Title Document

A POA grants **authority to act** — it does not transfer **ownership**. This distinction, as we will see in the Suraj Lamp ruling below, has enormous consequences for property transactions. The agent can sign a sale deed on your behalf, but a POA by itself cannot convey title to property.

## GPA vs SPA — When to Use Which

This is the most important practical decision every NRI faces when creating a POA. The wrong choice either grants too much power (dangerous) or too little (useless).

### General Power of Attorney (GPA)

A GPA grants **broad, sweeping authority** to the agent across multiple types of transactions. It typically covers all property matters, all banking transactions, all legal proceedings, and all administrative actions on behalf of the principal.

**When NRIs use GPA:**
- Managing multiple properties across different cities
- Handling all banking and investment operations
- Overseeing ongoing construction or development projects
- Managing affairs for elderly parents who may need to act on the NRI's behalf across various matters
- During extended absences with no planned return date

**Typical GPA scope includes:**
- Buy, sell, mortgage, lease, or manage any property
- Operate any bank account (NRE, NRO, FCNR)
- Sign all government documents, applications, and returns
- Appear before any court, tribunal, or government authority
- Execute all contracts and agreements
- Receive and disburse payments

**Risk level: HIGH.** A GPA essentially puts your entire Indian financial life in someone else's hands. If the agent goes rogue, the damage can be total.

### Special Power of Attorney (SPA)

An SPA grants **specific, limited authority** for a defined transaction or set of transactions. Once that transaction is completed, the SPA has served its purpose.

**When NRIs use SPA:**
- Selling a specific property (the most common NRI SPA use case)
- Purchasing a specific property
- Operating a specific bank account for a defined purpose
- Completing a specific tax filing or refund claim
- Representing in a specific legal proceeding
- Executing a specific mutual fund or demat transaction

**Typical SPA scope includes:**
- Sell property at [specific address] for a price not less than [amount]
- Execute the sale deed, receive consideration, and sign at the sub-registrar office
- Operate NRO Account No. [specific number] at [specific bank/branch]
- File income tax return for AY 2026-27

**Risk level: LOW to MODERATE.** The damage is bounded by the specific authority granted.

### Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | General Power of Attorney (GPA) | Special Power of Attorney (SPA) |
|---------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Scope | Broad, all-encompassing | Narrow, transaction-specific |
| Duration | Until revoked or death | Until specific task is completed |
| Risk of misuse | High | Low |
| Recommended for NRIs | Only when absolutely necessary | Strongly preferred |
| Property transactions | Covers all properties | Covers only named property |
| Bank operations | All accounts | Named account only |
| Registration required | Yes (for immovable property) | Yes (for immovable property) |
| Stamp duty | Higher in most states | Lower in most states |
| Revocability | Can be revoked anytime | Can be revoked anytime |

> **CA Mayank Wadhera, MKW Advisors:** *"In 95% of cases, I advise NRI clients to use an SPA, not a GPA. The GPA is a relic of an era when NRIs could not easily communicate with India. Today, with digital banking, video calls, and e-verification, there is rarely a legitimate need to hand over blanket authority. When I see a GPA, the first question I ask is: why does this need to be general?"*

## The Suraj Lamp Ruling — The Supreme Court Judgment Every NRI Must Know

**Suraj Lamp & Industries Pvt. Ltd. vs State of Haryana (2011) — Supreme Court of India, Civil Appeal No. 2065 of 2011**

This landmark three-judge bench ruling fundamentally changed how POAs interact with property transactions in India. If you execute a POA for property purposes and are not aware of this ruling, you are operating in dangerous territory.

### What the Court Held

The Supreme Court unequivocally held that:

1. **Property cannot be transferred through a GPA.** A sale of immovable property can only be effected through a registered sale deed under Section 54 of the Transfer of Property Act. A GPA, whether registered or unregistered, is not a conveyance instrument.

2. **GPA sales are not "sales" in law.** Transactions structured as "GPA + Agreement to Sell + Will" (a common practice in Delhi and other cities) do not convey title. The purchaser in such a transaction does not become the legal owner.

3. **Such transactions are not compulsorily registrable.** Since they are not conveyances, they cannot be registered as conveyances. The absence of registration means they create no rights in the property.

4. **The ruling is prospective.** Transactions completed before this judgment would not be reopened, but no new GPA-based property "transfers" would be recognized.

### What This Means for NRIs

**What a POA CAN do in a property sale:**
- Authorize the agent to execute and register a sale deed on behalf of the NRI seller
- Authorize the agent to receive the sale consideration on behalf of the NRI
- Authorize the agent to appear before the sub-registrar and sign all registration documents
- Authorize the agent to pay stamp duty, registration fees, and handle TDS compliance

**What a POA CANNOT do:**
- Transfer property title by itself (no matter how broadly worded)
- Serve as a substitute for a sale deed
- Create ownership rights in the agent or any third party
- Survive the death of the principal (the POA becomes void)

> **Practical implication:** If someone offers to "buy" your property through a GPA arrangement — where you sign a GPA in their favor, they pay you, and they take possession without a registered sale deed — that is NOT a valid sale. You remain the legal owner, they have no title, and both parties face legal and tax complications down the road.

## POA for Property Sale — The Most Common NRI Use Case

Selling property in India while living abroad is the primary reason NRIs execute POAs. Here is the complete process.

### What the SPA Must Contain

A valid SPA for property sale must include:

1. **Full identity of the Principal (NRI):** Name, passport number, overseas address, PAN, and Indian address (if any)
2. **Full identity of the Agent:** Name, Aadhaar number, PAN, address
3. **Complete property description:** Survey number, plot number, door number, area, boundaries (north, south, east, west), registration details, municipal ward
4. **Specific powers granted:**
   - Negotiate and finalize the sale price (specify minimum acceptable price)
   - Execute the sale deed before the sub-registrar
   - Receive sale consideration via cheque/RTGS into the NRI's NRO account
   - Deduct and deposit TDS under Section 195
   - Sign all ancillary documents (encumbrance certificate application, NOC from society/municipality, property tax clearance)
   - Appear before the sub-registrar for registration
   - Hand over original title documents to the buyer
5. **Limitations:** Maximum price below which the agent cannot sell, prohibition on gifting, prohibition on mortgaging
6. **TDS compliance clause:** The agent shall ensure the buyer deducts TDS at 20% (plus surcharge and cess) under Section 195 and deposits it with Form 26QC/27Q

### Step-by-Step Process

**Step 1: Draft the SPA**
Engage a lawyer familiar with Indian property law AND the specific state where the property is located. State-specific clauses matter — Karnataka, Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, and Delhi each have different registration requirements and stamp duty rules.

**Step 2: Execute and Notarize Abroad**
Sign the SPA before a Notary Public in your country of residence. The notary attests your signature and identity.

**Step 3: Attestation at Indian Consulate/Embassy**
Take the notarized SPA to the nearest Indian Embassy or Consulate for attestation. This step is critical — many sub-registrar offices in India will not accept a POA without consular attestation.

- Fee: Typically USD 10-25 per document
- Processing time: 2-5 working days (walk-in) or 7-14 days (postal service)
- Required documents: Original notarized SPA, passport copy, OCI/PIO card (if applicable), visa copy

**Step 4: Apostille (for Hague Convention Countries)**
If you are in a Hague Convention member country (US, UK, Canada, Australia, Germany, France, Singapore, and 120+ others), you may obtain an Apostille instead of consular attestation. The Apostille certifies the notary's authority and is accepted in India under the Convention.

**Step 5: Adjudication and Stamping in India**
Once the SPA arrives in India, it must be stamped with the appropriate stamp duty within the time limit (typically 3 months from the date of execution abroad, or within 3 months of arrival in India — varies by state). Submit to the Collector of Stamps for adjudication if required.

**Step 6: Registration (if for immovable property)**
Under Section 17(1)(b) of the Registration Act, 1908, a POA that authorizes the agent to deal with immovable property **must be registered** with the Sub-Registrar. An unregistered POA for property transactions is invalid and will be rejected.

**Step 7: Agent executes the sale**
With the registered SPA in hand, the agent can negotiate with the buyer, execute the sale deed, ensure TDS compliance, and receive the sale proceeds into the NRI's NRO account.

## POA for Property Purchase

NRIs buying property in India through a POA need to address FEMA compliance in addition to the standard POA requirements.

**Key points:**
- Payment must come from the NRI's NRE, NRO, or FCNR account — not the agent's account
- The property must be registered in the NRI's name, not the agent's
- The SPA should specifically authorize the agent to execute the purchase deed, pay stamp duty and registration fees from the NRI's designated account, and take possession
- Agricultural land, farmhouse, and plantation property cannot be purchased by NRIs (FEMA restriction) — a POA cannot circumvent this prohibition

## POA for Bank Operations (NRE/NRO Account Management)

Banks in India accept POAs for account operations, but with significant restrictions that have tightened considerably since 2020.

### What a POA Holder Can Typically Do

- Deposit cheques and cash
- Withdraw cash (subject to bank-specific limits, often capped at INR 50,000-1,00,000 per transaction)
- Transfer funds between the NRI's own accounts (NRE to NRO, etc.)
- Break or renew fixed deposits
- Request chequebooks and statements
- Update KYC documentation

### What a POA Holder Typically Cannot Do

- Open new accounts
- Add or change nominees
- Close accounts
- Transfer funds to third-party accounts (most banks restrict this)
- Apply for loans or credit facilities
- Activate internet banking or mobile banking
- Make international remittances

### Bank-Specific Requirements

Each bank has its own POA acceptance policy. SBI, HDFC, ICICI, Axis, and Kotak all have different POA formats and verification procedures. Many banks now require:

- The POA to be executed on the bank's own prescribed format
- In-person video verification of the NRI before accepting the POA
- A specific clause naming the bank, branch, and account number
- Re-verification of the POA every 1-3 years

> **CA Mayank Wadhera, MKW Advisors:** *"Before drafting a POA for bank operations, call your bank branch and ask for their specific POA requirements. I have seen perfectly valid, registered, apostilled POAs rejected because the bank wanted its own format or specific clauses. This is frustrating but entirely legal — banks are not obligated to accept any POA."*

## POA for Mutual Fund and Demat Operations

NRIs managing Indian investment portfolios often need a POA for:

- **Mutual fund transactions:** Purchase, redemption, switch, SIP registration/cancellation, folio consolidation, nomination changes
- **Demat account operations:** Buy/sell orders (through a broker), pledge/unpledge, corporate actions, dematerialization of physical shares
- **Portfolio Management Services (PMS):** Operational instructions, withdrawal requests

### Requirements

- Most AMCs (AMFI-registered mutual fund houses) accept registered POAs with specific mutual fund powers
- Demat depository participants (NSDL/CDSL) have standardized POA formats
- The POA must include the NRI's PAN, folio numbers or demat account numbers, and specific transaction types authorized
- SEBI regulations require re-confirmation of POA every 3 years for demat operations

## POA for Tax Filing

An NRI can authorize an agent through POA to:

- File income tax returns (ITR) on their behalf
- Respond to income tax notices and appear before the Assessing Officer
- Sign and submit Form 15CA/15CB for repatriation
- Apply for PAN or update PAN details
- File TDS returns (Form 26Q/27Q)
- Claim refunds and provide bank account details for refund credit

**Important:** The income tax e-filing portal allows authorized representatives to file returns using DSC (Digital Signature Certificate) or EVC (Electronic Verification Code). For NRIs, Aadhaar OTP-based e-verification is often not available (as Aadhaar may be linked to an Indian mobile number the NRI no longer uses). A CA can file as an authorized signatory under Section 140 of the Income Tax Act.

## Registration Requirements for NRI POA

### When Registration Is Mandatory

Under the Registration Act, 1908:

- **Section 17(1)(b):** Any POA that creates, declares, assigns, limits, or extinguishes any right, title, or interest in immovable property must be registered
- **Practical rule:** If the POA is to be used for any property transaction (sale, purchase, mortgage, lease for more than 1 year), it MUST be registered with the Sub-Registrar

### When Registration Is Optional (But Recommended)

- POA for bank operations only
- POA for movable property transactions (mutual funds, shares, vehicles)
- POA for tax filing only
- POA for legal representation only

Even when not mandatory, registration provides evidentiary value under Section 57 of the Registration Act — a registered document is presumed genuine until proven otherwise.

### Registration Process for POAs Executed Abroad

1. The NRI executes and notarizes the POA abroad
2. Gets it attested at the Indian Consulate/Embassy (or apostilled)
3. Sends the original to India
4. The agent presents the POA before the Sub-Registrar in the jurisdiction where the property is located
5. **Section 33 of the Registration Act** allows an agent holding a POA to present documents for registration
6. The Sub-Registrar verifies the consular attestation/apostille, stamps, and identity documents
7. Registration fee and stamp duty are paid
8. The document is entered into the registration records

**Time limit:** A POA executed abroad must be presented for registration within **3 months** of its receipt in India (extendable by up to 4 months with a condonation fee — varies by state).

## Notarization Abroad — Indian Consulate/Embassy Attestation

### The Process

1. **Prepare the POA document** (drafted by an Indian lawyer, incorporating state-specific requirements)
2. **Get it notarized** by a licensed Notary Public in your country of residence
3. **Book an appointment** with the Indian Embassy/Consulate (most consulates now require appointments for attestation services)
4. **Submit documents:**
   - Original notarized POA (some consulates require 2 copies)
   - Valid Indian passport (original + copy)
   - OCI/PIO card (if applicable)
   - Valid visa/residence permit in the country of residence
   - Passport-size photographs
   - Attestation fee (varies by consulate, typically USD 10-25 per document)
5. **Collect the attested POA** (2-5 business days for walk-in; 10-14 days for postal)

### Country-Specific Notes

- **United States:** Indian Consulates in New York, San Francisco, Chicago, Houston, Atlanta, and Washington DC. Some consulates offer Saturday services.
- **United Kingdom:** Indian High Commission in London. Walk-in attestation available on designated days.
- **UAE/Dubai:** Indian Consulate in Dubai, Embassy in Abu Dhabi. Heavy demand means longer wait times — book 2-3 weeks in advance.
- **Canada:** Indian Consulates in Toronto, Vancouver, and Ottawa.
- **Australia:** Indian Consulates in Sydney, Melbourne, and Canberra.
- **Singapore:** Indian High Commission. Typically 3-5 business days.

## Apostille Process for NRI POA

### What Is an Apostille?

An Apostille is a standardized certificate issued under the **Hague Convention Abolishing the Requirement of Legalisation for Foreign Public Documents (1961)**. It authenticates the origin of a public document (including notarized documents) for use in another member country.

India ratified the Hague Convention effective 14 July 2005. Documents apostilled in any member country are accepted in India without additional consular attestation.

### Step-by-Step Apostille Process

1. **Draft and sign** the POA
2. **Notarize** before a licensed Notary Public
3. **Submit for Apostille** to the designated authority in your country:
   - **US:** Secretary of State in the state where the notary is commissioned
   - **UK:** Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) — Legalisation Office
   - **Canada:** Global Affairs Canada (federal) or provincial authentication authority
   - **Australia:** Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT)
   - **UAE:** Ministry of Foreign Affairs and International Cooperation (MOFAIC)
   - **Singapore:** Singapore Academy of Law
4. **Receive the Apostille certificate** — a standardized form attached to or stamped on the document
5. **Send to India** — no consular attestation needed if apostilled

### Apostille vs Consular Attestation: Which to Choose?

| Feature | Apostille | Consular Attestation |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| Validity | Accepted in all Hague Convention countries | India-specific |
| Processing time | 1-5 business days (varies by country) | 3-14 business days |
| Cost | Typically lower (USD 5-15) | Typically higher (USD 10-25 + travel) |
| Availability | Widely available, often by mail | Requires consulate visit (or postal) |
| Acceptance in India | Universally accepted | Universally accepted |
| Recommended | Yes, where available | If apostille is unavailable or inconvenient |

## Revocation of Power of Attorney

A POA is not permanent. The principal can revoke it at any time, and certain events automatically terminate it.

### How to Revoke

1. **Execute a Revocation Deed:** A written instrument stating that the POA dated [date], executed in favor of [agent name], is hereby revoked with effect from [date]
2. **Serve notice on the agent:** Send the revocation deed to the agent by registered post/courier with acknowledgment
3. **Notify all relevant parties:** Banks, sub-registrar, mutual fund companies, demat depository, Income Tax department — anyone who has a copy of the original POA
4. **Register the revocation:** If the original POA was registered, the revocation should also be registered with the same Sub-Registrar office
5. **Publish a public notice:** Place a notice in a local newspaper (both English and vernacular) in the area where the property is located, informing the public that the POA stands revoked

### Automatic Termination Events

Under the Indian Contract Act, a POA automatically terminates upon:

- **Death of the principal:** The POA dies with the principal. The agent has no authority from the moment of death, even if unaware.
- **Death of the agent:** The POA cannot survive the agent's death.
- **Insanity of the principal:** If the principal becomes of unsound mind, the POA is void.
- **Insolvency of the principal:** In certain circumstances, insolvency terminates the agency.
- **Completion of the specific task:** An SPA terminates once the authorized transaction is completed.
- **Expiry of the stated term:** If the POA has a validity period (e.g., "valid for 2 years from the date of execution"), it expires automatically.
- **Mutual agreement:** Both parties agree to terminate.

### Irrevocable POA — A Special Case

Under Section 202 of the Indian Contract Act, a POA that is **coupled with interest** cannot be unilaterally revoked. This means if the agent has a personal interest or stake in the subject matter of the POA (for example, the agent is also a part-owner of the property), the principal cannot revoke it without the agent's consent.

For NRIs, this situation typically arises in joint property arrangements or where the agent has advanced money against the property.

## Risks of GPA — Misuse and Unauthorized Transactions

GPA misuse is one of the most common property fraud vectors in India. NRIs are disproportionately targeted because they are physically absent and unable to monitor day-to-day activities.

### Common Fraud Patterns

1. **Unauthorized sale:** The agent sells the property to a third party without the NRI's knowledge, pockets the sale proceeds, and disappears. By the time the NRI discovers the fraud, the buyer has taken possession.

2. **Mortgage fraud:** The agent mortgages the NRI's property with a bank or private lender, takes the loan amount, and defaults. The NRI's property faces auction.

3. **Encumbrance creation:** The agent creates a lease, license, or encumbrance on the property that reduces its value or prevents the NRI from selling.

4. **Multiple POA fraud:** The agent claims the POA has been lost and gets a duplicate, then uses both to create parallel transactions.

5. **Forgery of revocation:** The agent forges a revocation of the POA and re-executes a new POA in favor of an accomplice.

6. **Rent diversion:** The agent collects rent from the NRI's tenants and does not remit it.

### How to Protect Yourself

- **Always prefer SPA over GPA** — limit the scope to the specific transaction
- **Set a validity period** — do not create open-ended POAs
- **Include financial limits** — specify minimum sale price, maximum withdrawal amounts
- **Require dual signatures** — where possible, require the agent to act jointly with another trusted person
- **Monitor regularly** — check property encumbrance certificates, bank statements, and tax records every quarter
- **Register the POA** — even if not mandatory, registration creates an official record
- **Keep certified copies** — maintain copies of the POA with your lawyer in India
- **Use technology** — set up bank SMS alerts, enable transaction notifications, review AIS/26AS regularly

> **CA Mayank Wadhera, MKW Advisors:** *"I have handled cases where NRIs discovered their property had been mortgaged by a GPA holder only when the bank initiated foreclosure proceedings — years after the event. The solution is simple: never give a GPA unless you have absolute, unconditional trust in the agent, and even then, monitor the property through independent sources. An encumbrance certificate check every 6 months costs almost nothing and can save you crores."*

## Digital and Video POA Options in 2026

The legal landscape for digital documents in India is evolving, though POA execution remains largely traditional.

### Current Digital Options

1. **Digital Signature Certificate (DSC):** A POA can be signed using a Class 3 DSC issued by a Certifying Authority recognized under the Information Technology Act, 2000. However, registration offices across India have inconsistent acceptance of digitally signed POAs. Maharashtra and Karnataka have been more progressive; many other states still require physical signatures.

2. **Video-Based Customer Identification Process (V-CIP):** RBI introduced V-CIP in 2020 for KYC. Several banks now accept video verification of the NRI principal as part of POA validation. This does not replace the POA document itself but can substitute for in-person appearance.

3. **E-Stamping:** Most states now offer e-stamping through the Stock Holding Corporation of India Limited (SHCIL) platform. The POA can be executed on e-stamp paper purchased online, eliminating the need to procure physical stamp paper.

4. **Video Conferencing at Sub-Registrar:** Some states (notably Maharashtra under the IGR's initiative) allow parties to appear before the Sub-Registrar via video conference for document registration. This could potentially allow an NRI to execute the POA in real-time before the Sub-Registrar without physically visiting India.

5. **E-Registration:** Several states are piloting or implementing e-registration portals (KAVERI in Karnataka, iSarita in Maharashtra) that accept online submissions. However, the physical presentation of original documents is still required in most cases.

### Limitations

- **No fully digital POA yet:** As of March 2026, there is no pan-India mechanism for executing, notarizing, and registering a POA entirely online. Physical signatures, notarization, and original document presentation remain necessary in most states.
- **Blockchain-based registration:** The concept is being explored by UIDAI and some state governments, but no production implementation exists for POA.
- **Electronic POA (e-POA) for depository:** CDSL and NSDL accept electronic POA for demat operations, but this is specific to securities and does not extend to property or banking.

## Practical Template Sections for NRI POA

While the exact drafting should be done by a qualified lawyer, every NRI POA should include these sections:

### 1. Parties Clause
Full name, parentage (father's name), age, nationality, passport number, PAN, Aadhaar (if available), overseas residential address, and Indian address (if any) of both the Principal and the Agent.

### 2. Recital Clause
Background statement: the Principal is an NRI/OCI residing at [country], holding property/accounts/investments in India, and is unable to personally attend to matters due to overseas residence.

### 3. Grant of Authority Clause
Specific enumeration of every power granted. Be exhaustive but precise. Do not use vague phrases like "all matters whatsoever" — spell out each permitted action.

### 4. Property Schedule (for Property POA)
Complete legal description of the property: survey/plot number, door number, area in sq. ft./sq. meters, boundaries on all four sides, registration details (document number, book number, SRO), municipal details.

### 5. Financial Limits Clause
Minimum sale price, maximum single withdrawal, aggregate transaction limits, and prohibition on gifting, mortgaging, or pledging without separate written authorization.

### 6. Bank Account Details (for Banking POA)
Bank name, branch, account number, IFSC, and account type (NRE/NRO/FCNR). Specify whether the agent can only operate this specific account or all accounts.

### 7. TDS Compliance Clause
The agent shall ensure compliance with Section 195 TDS provisions, including obtaining the buyer's TDS deduction certificate and ensuring deposit of TDS within prescribed timelines.

### 8. Duration and Validity Clause
Start date, end date (or "until the completion of [specific transaction]"), and automatic expiry provisions.

### 9. Revocation Clause
The Principal reserves the right to revoke this POA at any time by written notice served on the Agent.

### 10. Indemnity Clause
The Agent shall indemnify and hold harmless the Principal against all losses, claims, and damages arising from the Agent's unauthorized or negligent acts.

### 11. Governing Law Clause
This POA is governed by the laws of the Republic of India, specifically the Power of Attorney Act, 1882, and the Indian Contract Act, 1872.

### 12. Attestation Block
Signatures of the Principal, two witnesses, Notary Public, and space for consular attestation/apostille stamp.

## Common Mistakes NRIs Make with POA

Having reviewed hundreds of NRI POA documents, these are the errors we see most frequently:

1. **Using a GPA when an SPA would suffice.** Overkill authority creates unnecessary risk.

2. **Not registering the POA for property transactions.** The sub-registrar will reject it, and you lose weeks while re-executing and re-registering.

3. **Not getting consular attestation or apostille.** A notarized-only POA executed abroad may not be accepted by Indian authorities.

4. **Missing state-specific stamp duty compliance.** An unstamped POA is inadmissible as evidence. Stamp deficiency can cause the entire transaction to be impounded.

5. **Vague property description.** "My flat in Mumbai" is not sufficient. The complete legal description including survey number, boundaries, and registration details is required.

6. **No expiry date.** An open-ended POA remains valid until revoked or the principal dies — creating long-term risk if you forget to revoke it.

7. **Wrong agent choice.** Choosing a family member for convenience rather than competence. The agent must be capable of navigating Indian bureaucracy — banks, sub-registrar offices, tax authorities.

8. **Not informing the bank separately.** Even with a valid, registered POA, you must submit it to the bank and obtain their acknowledgment before the agent can operate the account.

9. **Single copy.** Always make multiple notarized, attested copies. Banks, sub-registrars, and mutual fund companies may all need originals or certified copies.

10. **Not revoking after the task is done.** Once the property is sold or the specific task is completed, formally revoke the POA and notify all relevant parties.

11. **Assuming POA survives death.** It does not. If the principal dies, the POA is void from that moment. Estate planning requires a Will, not a POA.

12. **Not checking agent's track record.** Verify the agent does not have any legal disputes, criminal cases, or financial irregularities. A simple police verification and credit check can prevent disasters.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### 1. Can an NRI give POA to anyone, or must it be a relative?

There is no legal restriction on who can be appointed as an agent. The agent can be a relative, friend, colleague, or professional (lawyer or CA). However, banks and some government offices may informally prefer or specifically require the agent to be a close relative (spouse, parent, sibling, or child). Always check the institution's specific policy.

### 2. Is a POA executed abroad valid in India without registration?

A notarized and consular-attested (or apostilled) POA executed abroad is valid for movable property transactions, banking, tax filing, and legal representation without registration. However, for any transaction involving immovable property, the POA MUST be registered with the Sub-Registrar to be valid.

### 3. How long does an NRI POA remain valid?

A POA remains valid until it is revoked, the principal or agent dies, the specified task is completed (for SPA), or the stated validity period expires. There is no automatic expiry by operation of law — if you do not set a time limit and do not revoke, a GPA remains active indefinitely during the principal's lifetime.

### 4. Can a POA be given for agricultural land?

While a POA can technically be executed for any lawful purpose, NRIs cannot purchase agricultural land in India under FEMA. A POA cannot be used to circumvent this prohibition. However, if an NRI already owns agricultural land (acquired before becoming NRI or through inheritance), a POA can be given to manage or sell it.

### 5. Can two people be given POA for the same property?

Yes. A principal can appoint multiple agents and specify whether they must act jointly (all agents must agree on every action) or severally (any one agent can act independently). For NRIs, a joint POA with a requirement for both agents to sign provides better protection against misuse.

### 6. What happens to the POA if the NRI returns to India permanently?

The POA remains valid unless revoked. However, once the NRI returns to India and can personally handle affairs, there is no practical need for the POA. It is advisable to formally revoke the POA and notify all relevant parties to prevent any future misuse.

### 7. Can a POA holder file income tax returns for the NRI?

Yes. Under Section 140 of the Income Tax Act, a person duly authorized by the assessee can sign and verify the return. A POA holder or a Chartered Accountant with proper authorization can file the ITR on the NRI's behalf.

### 8. Is an unregistered GPA valid for any purpose?

An unregistered GPA is valid for purposes that do not involve immovable property — such as bank account operations, movable property transactions, legal representation, and administrative matters. However, it cannot be used for any immovable property transaction (sale, purchase, mortgage, lease over 1 year).

### 9. What is the cost of executing a POA for NRIs?

Typical costs include: lawyer drafting fee (INR 5,000-25,000 depending on complexity), notarization abroad (USD 10-50), consular attestation or apostille (USD 10-25), stamp duty in India (INR 50-500 depending on state), and registration fee (INR 500-2,000). Total cost is typically INR 15,000-40,000 (USD 175-475) end-to-end.

### 10. Can a POA be used to open a bank account for an NRI?

No. RBI and FEMA guidelines require the NRI to personally open NRE/NRO/FCNR accounts. Most banks require in-person KYC or video KYC for account opening. A POA can be used to operate an existing account but not to open a new one.

### 11. What if the agent misuses the POA?

The principal can file a criminal complaint (for cheating under Section 420 IPC or criminal breach of trust under Section 406 IPC — now Sections 318 and 316 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023), a civil suit for recovery and damages, and apply for an injunction to prevent further unauthorized transactions. Additionally, any transaction executed by the agent beyond the scope of the POA is void and not binding on the principal.

### 12. Can an OCI (Overseas Citizen of India) cardholder give POA in India?

Yes. OCI cardholders have the same rights as NRIs regarding property transactions and can execute POAs on identical terms. The OCI card should be specifically mentioned in the POA as an identity document alongside the passport.

### 13. Does a POA need to be renewed periodically?

There is no legal requirement for renewal. However, many banks and financial institutions treat POAs older than 3-5 years with suspicion and may request a fresh POA or a revalidation affidavit from the NRI. It is practical wisdom to renew the POA every 3 years.

### 14. Can a POA be executed electronically using e-signatures?

Under the Information Technology Act, 2000, electronic signatures (DSC) are legally valid for most documents. However, the Registration Act and Stamp Act have not been fully harmonized with the IT Act. As of 2026, most sub-registrar offices and banks prefer or require physical (wet) signatures on POA documents. The safest approach is to use a physical signature, notarize in person, and attest at the consulate.

## Final Checklist — Before You Execute an NRI POA

Use this checklist to ensure your POA is complete and compliant:

- [ ] Decided between GPA and SPA (SPA strongly recommended)
- [ ] Engaged a lawyer familiar with the specific Indian state's requirements
- [ ] Included complete property description (survey number, boundaries, registration details)
- [ ] Specified financial limits and prohibited actions
- [ ] Set a validity period or completion condition
- [ ] Included TDS compliance provisions (for property transactions)
- [ ] Signed before a Notary Public in your country of residence
- [ ] Obtained consular attestation or apostille
- [ ] Arranged for stamp duty payment in the correct Indian state within the time limit
- [ ] Registered the POA with the Sub-Registrar (mandatory for property transactions)
- [ ] Submitted the POA to the bank and obtained written acknowledgment
- [ ] Kept certified copies with your Indian lawyer, agent, and personal records
- [ ] Set a calendar reminder to monitor and eventually revoke the POA
